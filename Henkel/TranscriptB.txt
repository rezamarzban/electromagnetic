## 1. Skin Depth (δ)

**Starting Point:**  
For a plane wave propagating in a conductor with conductivity \(\sigma\) and permittivity \(\epsilon\), Maxwell’s equations lead to a complex wave number  
\[
k = \sqrt{j\omega\mu_0\left(\sigma+j\omega\epsilon\right)}.
\]

**Good Conductor Approximation:**  
At ELF frequencies and in good conductors, we assume \(\sigma \gg \omega\epsilon\). Then,
\[
k \approx \sqrt{j\omega\mu_0\sigma}.
\]

**Expressing \(k\) in Standard Form:**  
Writing \(k\) as
\[
k = (1+j)\sqrt{\frac{\omega\mu_0\sigma}{2}},
\]
the real part (which governs the exponential decay) is
\[
\operatorname{Re}(k)=\sqrt{\frac{\omega\mu_0\sigma}{2}}.
\]

**Definition of Skin Depth:**  
The skin depth \(\delta\) is defined as the distance over which the amplitude decays by \(1/e\), so:
\[
\delta = \frac{1}{\operatorname{Re}(k)} = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\omega\mu_0\sigma}}.
\]

## 2. Surface Impedance (\(Z_s\))

**Definition:**  
The surface impedance is defined as the ratio of the tangential electric field to the tangential magnetic field at the surface:
\[
Z_s = \frac{E_t}{H_t}.
\]

**General Expression:**  
For a plane wave decaying into a conductor,
\[
Z_s = \sqrt{\frac{j\omega\mu_0}{\sigma+j\omega\epsilon}}.
\]

**For a Good Conductor:**  
Again, using \(\sigma \gg \omega\epsilon\),
\[
Z_s \approx \sqrt{\frac{j\omega\mu_0}{\sigma}} = (1+j)\sqrt{\frac{\omega\mu_0}{2\sigma}}.
\]

**Magnitude:**  
The magnitude becomes
\[
|Z_s| = \sqrt{\frac{\omega\mu_0}{\sigma}},
\]
since \(|1+j| = \sqrt{2}\) cancels the factor in the square root.

## 3. Radiation Resistance of a Short Dipole (\(R_{r0}\))

**Standard Result:**  
For an electrically short dipole (i.e. \(l \ll \lambda\)), the radiated power is related to the current \(I\) and the dipole length \(l\). A standard result gives the free-space radiation resistance as:
\[
R_{r0} = 80\pi^2\left(\frac{l}{\lambda}\right)^2.
\]

**Explanation:**  
This formula results from calculating the far-field radiated fields, integrating the Poynting vector over a sphere, and equating the total radiated power to \(\frac{1}{2}I^2R_r\). The constant \(80\pi^2\) is characteristic for Hertzian (short) dipoles.

## 4. Far-Field Electric Field in the Earth-Ionosphere Waveguide

**Model Considerations:**  
In the Earth-ionosphere waveguide:
- The Earth (at \(z=0\)) acts as a nearly perfect conductor.
- The ionosphere is modeled as a boundary at \(z=h\) with an effective impedance.
- A buried horizontal electric dipole (HED) of length \(l_y\) radiates in this environment.

**Dipole Field in Free Space:**  
For a short dipole, the far-field electric field in free space has a dependence of
\[
E \propto \frac{j\eta_0\,k\,I\,l}{r}e^{-jkr}.
\]

**Modification for the Waveguide:**  
Within the waveguide, two adjustments occur:
1. **Cylindrical Propagation:** Instead of a spherical decay, the field spreads cylindrically. This is expressed by replacing \(1/r\) with the Hankel function \(H_0^{(2)}(kr)\) (which asymptotically behaves like \(\sqrt{2/(\pi kr)}e^{-j(kr+\pi/4)}\)).
2. **Ionospheric Coupling:** The effective height \(h\) of the ionosphere appears in the denominator (indicating that a higher ionosphere weakens the coupling) and an attenuation factor \(e^{-d/\delta}\) accounts for burial depth \(d\) versus skin depth \(\delta\).

**Resulting Expression:**  
Thus, the vertical electric field becomes
\[
E_z = \frac{j\,\eta_0\,k\,I\,l_y}{2\pi\,h}\; H_0^{(2)}(k\,r)\; e^{-d/\delta}.
\]

## 5. Reflection Coefficient at the Earth-Air Interface

**Basic Reflection Coefficient:**  
When a wave encounters an interface between two media with impedances \(Z_s\) (Earth) and \(\eta_0\) (free space), the reflection coefficient is given by:
\[
\Gamma = \frac{Z_s - \eta_0}{Z_s + \eta_0}.
\]

**Low Impedance Approximation:**  
If \(|Z_s| \ll \eta_0\), a first-order Taylor expansion yields:
\[
\Gamma \approx -1 + \frac{2Z_s}{\eta_0}.
\]

## 6. Adjusted Radiation Resistance Considering Earth's Impedance

**Context:**  
When the dipole is coupled to the Earth-ionosphere waveguide, the effective radiation resistance must account for the imperfect coupling at the Earth’s surface. The impedance mismatch (through \(\Gamma\)) and the waveguide geometry modify the radiation resistance from the free-space value.

**Derived Expression:**  
Based on a detailed analysis (which involves incorporating the reflection coefficient and the modified field distribution in the waveguide), one finds an expression of the form:
\[
R_r = \frac{Z_s^2\,\pi\,l_y^2}{4\,\eta_0\,h\,\lambda}.
\]

**Dimensional and Physical Check:**  
- **Numerator:** \(Z_s^2\,\pi\,l_y^2\) gives the scaling with the Earth’s surface impedance and the antenna’s effective length.
- **Denominator:** \(4\,\eta_0\,h\,\lambda\) reflects the coupling loss (through \(h\) and \(\lambda\)) and normalizes by the free-space impedance.
- **Physical Interpretation:**  
  - A higher \(Z_s\) (better coupling through the Earth’s surface) increases \(R_r\).  
  - A longer antenna (\(l_y\)) enhances radiation.  
  - A larger ionospheric height \(h\) or longer wavelength \(\lambda\) (lower frequency) reduces the effective radiation resistance, as expected in such a waveguide environment.
